Part 1: Design Principles

Functionality

Aesthetics mean nothing if your design cannot function

Be clear on your requirements before you start designing

Simplicity

Get your message across as simply as possible

This doesn't mean complex designs are bad

Usability

Use of the design should be intuitive

The less training required — the better

Flow

At first sight, your design should logically flow

It should be clear which section comes after which

Flow: Alignment

Keeping elements anchored to similar lines/items keeps your design grounded

This provides some organization

Flow: Hierarchy

Logically grouping items sections them off without explicit instruction

Demonstrates the level of relative importance

Flow: Balance

Elements should be distributed to prevent an overuse of one area

Don't be afraid of whitespace between elements

Coherence

The elements in your design should work together

They can be unique, but still appear grounded in a general theme

Coherence: Theme

Pick a theme for your design and stick with it

This theme should be appropriate for your goals/message

Coherence: Repetition

Repeat certain aspects of elements in multiple places in your design

This implicitly groups different elements, as long as your don't overdo it

Coherence: Modularity

Design elements to compose of other elements as much as possible

This will keep your designing simple and inherently coherent

Aesthetic

Your design should, put simply, look good

While there are a range of tastes/looks, looking "good" has some general guidelines

Aesthetic: Color

Pick a primary color and a secondary color that complement each other and go from there

Pick colors that highlight your message while still differing from each other

Aesthetic: Type

Pick a main font that is simple and easily readable

Pick a heading/display font that fits your theme more and use this less

Aesthetic: Expression

Design your elements with your theme in mind

A more neutral theme should have simpler element, and vice versa

Breaking Rules

  1. Never use a metaphor, simile, or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.
  2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
  3. If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
  4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
  5. Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
  6. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

George Orwell

Part 2: Design Process

1. Formalize Your Message and Goals

What do you want to convey?

What is the overarching goal of your design?

2. Pick a Theme

What should your overall design look like?

This should reflect and support your message/goals

3. Determine the User Experience

List everything you want the user to be able to do

Don't consider specific design now, just things the user wants

4. Specify Your Requirements

What are the requirements you, as the designer, are following?

Do you have any limitations, constraints, or other needs that don't directly concern the user?

5. Outline Your Elements

Create a rough outline of what elements you'll have and how they are organized

The main point of this is to get an idea of what elements you need to start with

6. Iterate on Your Elements

Design them, focusing on meeting the requirements and the UX for each specific element

Refine repeatedly, following design principles

7. Ensure Coherence and Flow

Put the elements together and see how they work together

Fix any issues with flow or coherence and iterate

8. Test

Get fresh eyes to look it over and find pain points that you might not see

You want to understand how someone who knows nothing about your design interacts with it

9. Repeat 6-8

Keep going until you find a design both you and your users are happy with

This could take a while, but you don't need perfection

10. Profit

Part 3: Practice

Dog Adoption Form

One page only

Should have all the info required to pick a dog to adopt

Should collect all the adopter's information

Should provide a convincing case to adopt each dog

Run by a newer clinic that focuses on puppies and targets a younger audience (12-25)

Part 4: A Few More Things

Fonts

Make sure you check the license of the font you're using

Google Fonts is a great place to get freely useable fonts

Other good freely useable fonts are Inter, Manope, Iosevka

Colors and Contrast

When picking colors, ensure that the contrast between them is sufficient enough

Use something like coolors' constrast checker
